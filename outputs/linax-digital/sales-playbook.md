# Linax Digital — Sales Playbook

**Version 1.0 — 2026-04-27**
**Target personas:** Mike Hernandez (Scaling Contractor), James Caldwell (Legacy Operator), Marcus Johnson (Growth-Mode Franchise Operator)
**Stages covered:** Prospecting → Discovery (Free Audit) → Post-Audit Follow-Up → Close

---

## 1. How to use this playbook

This is an internal operating manual for a founder-led, audit-first sales motion. You're the only rep. That changes how this is built:

- **Optimized for one person.** No SDR/AE handoffs. No CRM rituals you don't have time for. The cadences assume one human running everything.
- **Voice-checked.** Every customer-facing line in here — emails, scripts, objection responses — was written to match `writing-voice.md`. Don't paraphrase into agency-speak.
- **A living doc.** Update objections as you hear new ones. Replace placeholder proof points with real numbers as client wins land. Set a quarterly review (first Monday of Jan / Apr / Jul / Oct).

**Read order on a new prospect:**
1. Persona snapshot (Section 4) → which one are they closest to?
2. Stage section for where you are in the deal
3. Objection library (Section 9) right before any call

## 2. The Linax Digital sales motion

The motion is built around five non-negotiable rules. They're also the reasons you win.

| Rule | What it looks like in practice |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **Free audit before any pitch.** | Every prospect gets a Free Digital Presence Audit before a proposal exists. The audit is the discovery. The audit is the demo. The audit is the trust-builder. No exceptions. |
| **Transparent pricing on the website.** | Don't dance around price on calls. Prices are public. Reference the page if asked. The transparency is the differentiator — don't undercut it by going coy. |
| **Founder-direct.** | You're the person they're buying. Don't pretend otherwise. "I build everything myself" is a closer line, not a weakness. |
| **No fake urgency.** | No "spots filling up." No "price going up Friday." If the deal is right, it'll close. If it's not, urgency theater costs trust. |
| **You'll tell them when it's not a fit.** | When someone's not a fit, say so on the call. It costs you the deal and earns you the referral. |

### The four services and how they bundle

| Service | Entry price | Typical bundle path |
|---------|------------|--------------------|
| Website | $3,000 lump or $180/mo (12-mo) + $25/mo hosting | Foundation play — almost every new client starts here or with Reputation Mgmt |
| SEO Foundation | $600 setup + $300/mo | Layered after the website is live |
| Ads | $300/mo min or 10% of spend (cap $2K/mo) + ROAS guarantee | Layered when prospect needs leads now |
| Reputation Management | $195/mo | Lowest-friction entry point. SaaS-feel without SaaS handoff. |

**The Reputation Management onramp.** Land on RepMgmt. Demonstrate the platform. Expand into website, SEO, Ads. This is the deliberate strategy — RepMgmt is cheap, fast to deliver, and shows the platform daily. It's also the most "tool-shaped" service you sell, which makes it Rachel Torres territory (champion path into Mike/James shops).

## 3. Qualification framework

Use this on every discovery call. It's BANT adapted for local SMBs — same logic, less corporate.

### The four checks

| Check | What you're confirming | Green flag | Red flag |
|-------|----------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Real business** | Revenue, tenure, vertical fit | $500K+ revenue, 2+ years operating, in target verticals (home services, marine, professional, retail-with-service) | Pre-revenue, side hustle, or pure ecommerce/national brand |
| **Real budget** | They can afford the bundle that matches their need | Already paying an agency or platform; or has marketing line in P&L | "I just want a quick site for $500" / "I'm not paying monthly for anything" |
| **Real authority** | Decision-maker is on the call (or one call away) | Owner/operator on the audit call | Office manager who "needs to check with the owner" — this is fine but means add a stage |
| **Real urgency** | Something is forcing the conversation now | Slow season they want to fix, lost a big competitor's referral, hurricane-recovery work tapering, new location | "Just exploring options" with no pain — usually a tire-kicker |

### Anti-persona — walk away or refer out

These are the deals that look good on paper and burn months. Spot them early.

- Sophisticated in-house marketing/tech team (they need a specialist, not a generalist)
- National brand or business scaling beyond SWFL
- Won't share access to GBP, ad accounts, or website backend
- "Just a logo" or business-card-only requests
- Wants a one-time cheap site with zero relationship after
- A prospect who's price-shopped 5+ agencies before you — they're hunting the lowest bid, not the right partner

**The graceful exit line:**

> "Honestly, I don't think I'm the right fit for what you're describing. You'd be better served by [referral / recommendation]. If anything changes and you want a different kind of partner, you know where to find me."

This costs nothing, earns referrals, and protects your time.

## 4. Buyer personas — the top three

You sell to seven personas in the long run. Three are worth most of your sales energy in the founding-client phase. Memorize these three.

### 4.1 Mike Hernandez — The Scaling Contractor

> **One-line:** A 35–55 year old HVAC, plumbing, roofing, or remodeling owner doing $750K–$3M who's been burned by an agency and has 60 reviews when his competitor has 300.

**Snapshot**

| | |
|--|--|
| Revenue | $750K–$3M |
| Title | Owner-operator |
| Team | 2–15 employees |
| Vertical | HVAC, plumbing, roofing, remodeling, garage door, epoxy, electrical |
| Tech savvy | Low-medium. Uses Google Maps daily. Suspicious of dashboards. |
| Decision speed | Fast once trust is established. Slow to start trusting. |
| Typical close | Growth Managed bundle, $1,997/mo. Sometimes starts with website then expands. |

**Where you find him**
- Already running Google Ads (poorly) — visible in auction insights
- 50–150 Google reviews while competitors have 200–400
- Website is a WordPress template from 2019 with stock photos
- Has "Family Owned & Operated" or "Licensed & Insured" in his GBP description
- Active in local trade associations, BNI chapters, Chamber of Commerce
- Referred by a current happy client (best channel for him)

**What he cares about**
- The phone ringing with quality leads, not Angi spam
- Showing up #1 for "[service] near me" in Cape Coral / Fort Myers / Naples
- Not getting ripped off again
- Not having homework

**What he doesn't care about**
- The technology stack
- Marketing strategy frameworks
- "Brand storytelling"
- Any deliverable that looks like a PDF report

**The opener that works**

> "I pulled up [his business] on Google Maps yesterday. You've got 4.8 stars and 73 reviews — really solid. But you're showing up fourth for 'AC repair Cape Coral' behind two competitors with worse reviews than you. Want to know why?"

**The opener that doesn't**

> "Hi! I'd love to discuss how Linax Digital can help you leverage cutting-edge digital marketing to grow your business." (You will be ignored.)

**Recommended path**
1. Free Digital Presence Audit (always)
2. Website rebuild ($3,000 lump or $180/mo) + GBP optimization (part of SEO Foundation)
3. Reputation Management ($195/mo) layered immediately — fixes the review gap
4. Ads when he needs leads in 2 weeks ($300/mo + ad spend)
5. Bundle into Growth Managed (~$1,997/mo) once trust is built

**Watch-outs**
- He'll tell you a story about being burned by an agency. Listen. Don't defend the industry. Just say "yeah, that happens — let me show you what I do differently."
- He hates feeling stupid. Never use a term you haven't translated.
- He'll want to talk on the phone, not over email.

**Signals you're winning**
- He stops asking about price and starts asking about timeline
- He asks if you've worked with anyone in his trade
- He volunteers a story about a competitor he wants to beat

### 4.2 James Caldwell — The Legacy Operator

> **One-line:** A 50–65 year old second-generation owner of a $2M–$10M roofing or remodeling shop with a 25-year reputation and a 2014 website.

**Snapshot**

| | |
|--|--|
| Revenue | $2M–$10M |
| Title | Owner / 2nd-gen owner |
| Team | 10–40 employees |
| Vertical | Established roofing, remodeling, custom builders, marina services, generational service businesses |
| Tech savvy | Low. Delegates digital. |
| Decision speed | Slow. Conservative. Wants to think about it. |
| Typical close | Full-Service Managed bundle, $3,500–$5,000/mo. Starts with a high-touch website project. |

**Where you find him**
- Has been in business 15–40 years
- Trucks/vans are his marketing — visible everywhere in the territory
- Existing website built by his nephew or a regional WordPress shop in 2014
- Strong word-of-mouth pipeline that's plateauing as referral sources retire
- Active in Rotary, country clubs, chamber boards
- Often has a daughter/son in the business who pushes for "modernizing"

**What he cares about**
- Protecting the family-name brand
- Beating younger, more digital-savvy competitors
- High-ticket projects, not lead volume
- Being treated as a peer, not a "small business owner"
- Slow, deliberate decisions he can defend to his partners/kids

**What he doesn't care about**
- Speed of execution
- Being "innovative" or "cutting-edge"
- Dashboards or self-service portals
- Anything that feels like a tech startup

**The opener that works**

> "I've been driving past [his trucks] for years — the brand's recognizable, you've clearly built something. But the website hasn't kept up with the trucks. The last [real customer name from his market] I worked with had the same disconnect. Mind if I show you what I'd do?"

**The opener that doesn't**

> "Your website looks dated and your SEO is underperforming." (Insulting his life's work. Dead deal.)

**Recommended path**
1. In-person or video Free Digital Presence Audit (he prefers face-to-face; honor that)
2. Premium website project, $3,000+ — pitched as a multi-month engagement, not a transaction
3. SEO Foundation + Reputation Management layered after launch
4. Ads layered conservatively — he distrusts paid media at first
5. Build to Full-Service Managed at $3,500–$5,000/mo over 6–12 months

**Watch-outs**
- He may be testing you with one project before committing to anything ongoing. Pass the test by overdelivering on phase one.
- He may have a relative who "does the website." Don't position against them — position alongside. "I work with what's already there when it makes sense."
- His decision cycle is 4–12 weeks. Don't push a 7-day close.
- If you mention "AI-augmented delivery" the wrong way, he'll think it's gimmicky. Lead with outcomes; AI is internal context only.

**Signals you're winning**
- He brings his spouse or partner to a follow-up meeting
- He asks about your other clients in his vertical/peer group
- He shares something off-the-record about a competitor or industry trend

### 4.3 Marcus Johnson — The Growth-Mode Franchise Operator

> **One-line:** A multi-location service operator (3–12 locations) doing $2M–$10M+ who needs repeatable per-location marketing and unified reporting.

**Snapshot**

| | |
|--|--|
| Revenue | $2M–$10M+ |
| Title | Franchise owner, multi-unit operator, regional manager |
| Team | 25–150+ across locations |
| Vertical | Multi-unit franchises (home services, fitness, food, automotive), regional service brands, expanding service businesses |
| Tech savvy | Medium-high. Uses Excel, dashboards, sometimes a basic CRM. |
| Decision speed | Methodical. Wants a pilot before scaling. |
| Typical close | Pilot at one location → Managed bundle per location, $1,500–$2,500/mo each. Highest-ceiling deal type. |

**Where you find him**
- 3+ locations on Google Maps, all with separate GBP listings (often inconsistent)
- Separate websites or location pages with mismatched branding
- LinkedIn presence — easier to research and outbound to than Mike or James
- Industry associations for his franchise system
- Often referred by a corporate office or fellow franchisee

**What he cares about**
- Per-location visibility and accountability
- Repeatability — what works at one site working at all sites
- Rollup reporting he can show to his partners or franchisor
- ROI per location, not just aggregate
- Not having to manage marketing himself or hire a CMO

**What he doesn't care about**
- Cute design touches
- Bespoke per-location creative (he wants consistency, not custom)
- Long brand storytelling
- Anything that doesn't scale

**The opener that works**

> "You've got [X] locations on Google Maps. Three of them are ranking well, two are invisible, and one is getting destroyed by [competitor]. The fix is the same playbook applied consistently across all of them. Want to see what that looks like for [his weakest location]?"

**The opener that doesn't**

> "We can build you a custom website." (He doesn't need custom. He needs replication.)

**Recommended path**
1. Free Digital Presence Audit on **all locations** — this is the demo
2. Pilot at the weakest location: Managed bundle with website + SEO + RepMgmt (~$1,500–$2,000/mo)
3. 90-day proof window with weekly reporting
4. Roll out per-location bundles across remaining sites
5. Long-term: $10K–$25K+/mo recurring across all locations

**Watch-outs**
- He'll want a pilot. Honor it. Don't try to close the whole portfolio on day one.
- Reporting is a deal driver. Build him a simple per-location rollup early.
- He may have a franchisor with brand restrictions. Ask about this on the audit call.
- He's the highest-ceiling deal you'll see, but also the most operationally complex. Be honest with yourself about whether you can deliver before you sell it.

**Signals you're winning**
- He asks for the pilot to start at a specific location (he's mentally committed)
- He introduces you to a partner, GM, or fellow franchisee
- He asks how reporting will work across locations

## 5. Stage 1 — Prospecting

Your prospecting mix as a founder-led shop, ranked by ROI:

| Channel | Why it works for you | Effort |
|--------|---------------------|--------|
| **Referrals from happy clients** | Highest close rate. Only scalable once you have 5+ delighted clients. | Low — but requires the work upfront |
| **Targeted cold outreach (audit-first)** | Specific, observation-led. Not spray-and-pray. | Medium — 30–60 min research per prospect |
| **Local networking (BNI, Chamber, vertical associations)** | Slow but compounds. Especially good for James. | Medium — recurring time investment |
| **Inbound via website (free audit form)** | Will grow as SEO does. Currently small but high-quality. | Low ongoing — site does the work |
| **Content / case studies** | Builds authority. Best after first wins are in. | High effort, slow payoff |

The unfair-advantage channel for a one-person shop is **#2 — targeted cold outreach with a specific observation**. The alternative ("Hi, we do SEO") gets ignored. Specific observations earn replies.

### Pre-call research checklist — the audit before the audit

Before any first contact, spend 20–40 minutes on this. The output is one specific, true, useful observation you can lead with.

- [ ] Pull their **Google Business Profile**. Note: review count vs. top 3 competitors, primary category, photos last updated, response rate to reviews
- [ ] Pull their **website**. Run it through PageSpeed Insights. Note: load time, mobile usability, CMS (WordPress / Squarespace / etc.), last visible blog post date
- [ ] Search "[their service] [their city]" — **where do they rank?** Map Pack? Organic? Nowhere?
- [ ] Check **Meta Ad Library** for their domain — running ads or not?
- [ ] Pull 2–3 **direct competitors** by the same search. Note where they're winning.
- [ ] Find one **specific, fixable thing** to lead with. Not five. One.

That one specific thing becomes the opener.

### Phone / DM scripts

Same logic, shorter. If you have a real referral name, use it.

> "Hi [Name] — this is [you] with Linax Digital. [Referrer] mentioned you'd been thinking about your website. I've been looking at your Google listing this morning — there's a couple things that stand out. Got 60 seconds, or should I call back?"

If they say keep going:

> "Two things. One, your reviews are stronger than the businesses ranking above you. That's a win you're not getting credit for. Two, your website is loading in [N] seconds on mobile, which is killing you with anyone searching from a phone. I do free audits for SWFL businesses — would it be useful to walk through it together?"

### Cold-outreach cadence (for one founder)

Realistic single-founder volume: 5–10 prospects/week, deeply researched.

| Day | Touch |
|-----|-------|
| 1 | Cold email (specific observation) |
| 4 | Phone call — voicemail referencing the email |
| 8 | Second email — different angle, same prospect (review gap, website speed, competitor doing X) |
| 14 | Final touch — short, low-pressure, "leaving you my notes from the audit anyway" |
| 30+ | Drop into long-term nurture (newsletter, referral if they come up, no further direct outreach) |

If three touches don't get a response, **stop**. They saw it. Not a fit right now. Don't burn the bridge.

## 6. Stage 2 — Discovery (the Free Digital Presence Audit Call)

The audit call is the discovery call is the first demo. Treat it as your most important sales asset — because it is.

### Pre-call (the morning of)

- [ ] Re-pull GBP, website performance, search rankings (5 min — things change)
- [ ] Have one printed or screen-shared **one-pager** with the specific observations you'll walk through
- [ ] Have the pricing page open in a tab — you'll reference it
- [ ] Have your calendar open for booking the next step before you hang up

### The 45-minute audit call structure

| Minutes | Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 0–5 | Welcome + agenda + their context | Confirm who's on the call, what they want from it, what's been going on |
| 5–20 | Discovery questions (see below) | Understand the real business, the real pain, the real budget |
| 20–35 | The audit walkthrough | Show — don't tell — the 3–4 specific things you found |
| 35–40 | Map findings to services | "Here's what I'd do, in order, and what it costs" |
| 40–45 | Next step | Book the follow-up, send the recap, or close on the spot if they're ready |

### Discovery questions — by topic

Don't ask all of these. Pick the ones that fit the persona and where the conversation goes. Listen more than you talk. Take notes — they'll show up in the recap email.

**The business itself**
- "How long have you been at this?"
- "What's the typical job for you — size, type of customer?"
- "Walk me through your busy season vs. slow season."

**Current marketing**
- "What's working right now to get the phone ringing?"
- "Where do most of your jobs come from — referrals, search, ads, repeat?"
- "What have you tried in the last couple years that didn't work? What happened?"

**The pain**
- "If a magic wand fixed one thing about your marketing tomorrow, what would it be?"
- "What's your single biggest frustration with how things are right now?"
- "Have you worked with an agency before? How did that go?" *(Always ask this. Always.)*

**Decision and budget**
- "When you've made a decision like this in the past, who's involved?"
- "What's a reasonable monthly budget for marketing for a business your size — what feels right to you?" *(Better than asking for a number directly.)*
- "If we did this, when would you want it live?"

**Authority and process**
- "Anyone else who'd weigh in on this with you?"
- "Once we talk, what's the next step typically look like for you?"

### Persona-specific pivots

| Persona | Lean harder on | Avoid |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| Mike | The agency-burn story; his competitors specifically; review gap | Strategy frameworks; "brand"; long timelines |
| James | His brand legacy; high-ticket projects; peer competitors | Volume metrics; cheap/fast framing; tech jargon |
| Marcus | Per-location performance; rollup reporting; pilot scope | One-off custom work; small-scale solutions |

### The audit walkthrough

Pick **3–4 things** you found in pre-call research. Walk through them in plain English. For each:

1. **Show what you found** ("Your site loads in 7 seconds on mobile. Here it is.")
2. **Explain what it costs them** ("Half the people clicking from a phone will leave before it loads.")
3. **Say what fixes it** ("A rebuild on a modern stack. About 3 weeks. $3,000 lump or $180/month.")

Don't try to fix everything in the audit. Three or four real findings beats a 20-page "comprehensive analysis" every time.

### Mapping findings to services

After the walkthrough, summarize:

> "Here's how I'd actually sequence this if you wanted me to do it — what I'd do first, second, third — and roughly what it costs. You don't have to decide today. I'll send all of this in writing."

Then: literally do that. Sequence + price + outcome, on the call.

| If the pain is | Lead with |
|---------------|-----------|
| "I need leads now" | Ads + GBP optimization (fast wins) |
| "My website is embarrassing" | Website project + RepMgmt for credibility |
| "I'm losing reviews to a competitor" | RepMgmt as standalone, $195/mo entry |
| "I don't show up on Google" | SEO Foundation + GBP work |
| "I'm juggling too many tools" | RepMgmt as the consolidator |

### The next step — never end without one

Before you hang up, one of these must be true:
1. They've signed up on the call (rare but happens)
2. The next call is on the calendar (proposal review, technical deep-dive, partner intro)
3. They've agreed to a specific timeline ("I'll send the proposal Tuesday, you'll review by Friday, we'll talk Monday")

"I'll think about it and get back to you" is not a next step. If you hear it, gently ask: "Totally fair — what's the right timeline for that, and should I check in next [day] or wait for you?"

### Common discovery traps

- **The data dump.** If you talked for 30 of 45 minutes, you ran a webinar, not a discovery call.
- **The premature pitch.** Pitching before you've heard the pain means you're guessing what to pitch.
- **The free consulting trap.** Sharing every finding in detail means they don't need to hire you. Show enough to prove you know your stuff. Hold the implementation behind the engagement.
- **The "send me a proposal" trap.** If you haven't qualified budget and authority, the proposal is going in a folder labeled "Marketing Things." Confirm both before drafting anything.

## 7. Stage 3 — Post-Audit Follow-Up

This is where most agency deals die. Yours won't, because the follow-up is where you separate from generalists.

### Within 24 hours of the audit call

Send the **recap email** (template below). Three things must be in it:
1. The specific findings from the audit (so they can show their partner/spouse)
2. The recommended sequence + price
3. The agreed-upon next step

### The audit recap email template

> **Subject:** Notes from our call — [Business name]
>
> Hi [Name] —
>
> Quick recap from [day]'s call so you've got everything in writing.
>
> **What I found**
> 1. [Finding 1, in one line. Plain English.]
> 2. [Finding 2.]
> 3. [Finding 3.]
> 4. [Finding 4 if relevant.]
>
> **What I'd do, in order**
> - First: [Service] — [why first, expected timeline, price]
> - Then: [Service] — [why second, expected timeline, price]
> - Optional: [Service] — [if applicable]
>
> **Total monthly:** [dollar figure if recurring]
> **Total upfront:** [dollar figure if any]
>
> **The guarantees:**
> - Free audit (this), no commitment
> - Month-to-month on SEO, Ads, RepMgmt — cancel anytime
> - 2:1 ROAS guarantee on Ads — drops below, no management fee
>
> Happy to answer questions over email or jump on another call. If you want to move forward, I'll send a short agreement and we can have [first deliverable] live by [realistic date].
>
> [Founder name]
> [Phone]

### When the proposal is its own document

For James and Marcus, the recap email may be too short. Send the email plus a 4–6 page proposal with:
- One-page exec summary (their challenge, your approach, expected outcome)
- Findings detail
- Service plan + timeline
- Pricing
- Next steps

Use Section 11's proposal template.

### Handling "I need to think about it"

Almost everyone says this. The response is short and doesn't push:

> "Totally fair. What's helpful for you to think through it — anything I haven't answered, or just internal time? Either way, I'll send the recap today and check in [specific day] unless I hear from you sooner."

Two things this does:
1. Shows you're not pressuring — earns trust
2. Sets the next concrete touchpoint, so the deal doesn't drift

### When to follow up after the recap

| Day after recap | Touch |
|----|-------|
| 0 | Send recap email |
| 3 | Short check-in — "Wanted to see if you've had a chance to look. No rush. Happy to answer anything." |
| 7 | Add value — share a relevant case study, a competitor screenshot, or a market observation. Not a "just following up." |
| 14 | One more nudge — "Should I assume timing isn't right and circle back in a quarter, or are you still looking at it?" |
| 30 | If no response, drop into long-term nurture |

### Multi-stakeholder selling — when Rachel is the champion

Sometimes the office manager (Rachel persona) is the one on the audit call, and the owner (Mike or James) is the actual buyer. Treat Rachel as your champion, not your blocker.

**What Rachel needs from you to sell internally:**
- A one-page summary the owner can read in 90 seconds
- A short answer to "what's it cost and what do we get?"
- One sentence she can repeat: "It's the audit guy who built the site for [neighbor's business]."
- An offer to talk to the owner directly when she's ready

**The arming-the-champion email:**

> Hi Rachel —
>
> Thanks for the call. Attached is a one-pager you can show [Owner name]. Three things I'd flag if I were in your seat:
>
> 1. [Most important finding, one line]
> 2. [Cost + first deliverable timeline]
> 3. [Specific competitor or market reason it matters now]
>
> If [Owner] wants to talk to me directly before deciding, I'm happy to grab 20 minutes with him. Whatever's easiest.
>
> [Founder name]

## 8. Stage 4 — Close

Closing in this motion isn't pressure. It's clarity. By the time you're at close, the prospect has:
- Seen the audit
- Read the recap
- Possibly reviewed a proposal
- Asked you their questions

Your job at close is to remove the last friction and get a signature.

### The "are we doing this" call

Sometimes the recap email closes the deal directly — they reply yes, you send the agreement. More often, you need one more call. Keep it short (15–20 minutes).

**Structure:**

| Minutes | Section |
|---------|---------|
| 0–3 | "Where are you on it? What's left for you to decide?" |
| 3–10 | Address the remaining objections (Section 9) |
| 10–15 | Walk through the agreement, payment, and start date |
| 15–20 | Send the agreement on the call. Set the kickoff. |

### Final-stage objections

These come up at close, not earlier. Treat them differently from discovery objections — they're about commitment, not understanding.

| Objection | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "Can we wait until [next month / next quarter]?" | "We can. Two questions — what changes between now and then? And: what does waiting cost you in jobs you're not getting in the meantime?" |
| "Can you start without a contract?" | "I can't, but the contract is short and you can cancel anytime on the recurring services. Want me to walk you through it?" |
| "Can I get a discount?" | "Pricing's published on the website — same price for everyone, which is part of why people trust it. What I can do is sequence things to spread the upfront cost." |
| "I want to talk to one of your other clients first." | "Absolutely — let me reach out to [specific client] and see if they'll take 10 minutes with you. I'll connect you directly." *(Have this list ready. Update it.)* |
| "I'm not ready to commit to monthly." | "Fair. Start with the website project — that's a one-time engagement. We can talk about ongoing services after launch when you can see what's working." |

### Contract / SOW

Keep the agreement short — 2–3 pages max for SMB services. Cover:
- Scope (what you'll deliver, in plain English)
- Timeline (start date, milestones, launch)
- Payment (amount, schedule, method)
- Cancellation terms (month-to-month except website subscription)
- Asset ownership (clarify upfront — they own their domain and content)

Send via DocuSign or HelloSign. Don't email a PDF and ask for a printed signature. Friction kills closes.

### Welcome and onboarding handoff

Once signed, send a kickoff email within 4 hours.

> **Subject:** Welcome — kicking off [project / service]
>
> [Name] —
>
> Glad we're working together. Here's what happens next:
>
> 1. **30-minute kickoff call:** [Calendly link] — pick any time this week
> 2. **Access I'll need:** [specific list — GBP, ads accounts, domain registrar, etc.]
> 3. **First deliverable:** [Specific thing, by specific date]
>
> One person handles everything — me. So the inbox you reply to is the same inbox the work gets done from. If something needs my eye, just hit reply.
>
> Welcome aboard.
>
> [Founder name]

### Why deals stall, and how to revive them

| Why it stalled | Reactivation move |
|---------------|------------------|
| Owner went out of town / busy season hit | Light touch every 2 weeks. Don't push. Wait for the calm. |
| A competitor pitched them after you | Ask directly: "Are you considering someone else? Genuinely happy to tell you when they're a better fit." |
| They got cold feet on price | Re-anchor on what they're losing by waiting. Offer to start smaller (RepMgmt at $195/mo as a wedge). |
| Internal disagreement | Offer a 20-minute call with the dissenting partner. Address the concern directly. |
| You overshot — wrong tier | Re-propose smaller. "On reflection, here's a leaner version that gets you the first wins." |

A deal isn't dead until the prospect tells you it is. But also: a deal that's been stalled 60+ days without movement usually isn't coming back this quarter. Move it to long-term nurture and free up your calendar.

## 9. Objection library

Pulled from the existing product-marketing context, expanded with what they really mean, the talk track, the proof, and the follow-up question that keeps the conversation moving.

### Format

Each objection has five parts:
- **Objection** — exactly how you'll hear it
- **What they really mean** — the actual concern beneath the words
- **Response** — the talk track (voice-checked)
- **Proof** — the specific evidence
- **Follow-up question** — what to ask next to keep moving

---

#### "I've been burned by agencies before — how are you different?"

**What they really mean:** "I'm scared to spend money again. Convince me you're not going to disappear after the contract is signed."

**Response:**

> "Yeah, that's most people I talk to. The honest answer is I build everything myself — no account managers, no offshore production, no handoffs. The person you're talking to right now is the person designing your site, running your ads, and answering when something breaks. If something isn't working, I'm the one who fixes it. That's the difference."

**Proof:** Founder-led delivery. Same email and phone for sales, support, and execution. Reference an existing client where the founder did the work end-to-end.

**Follow-up:** "What specifically went wrong with the last one? I want to make sure we don't repeat it here."

---

#### "How much does this cost?"

**What they really mean:** "I want to know if I can afford this before I invest more time."

**Response:**

> "Prices are on the website — no 'contact us for a quote' games. SEO starts at $600 setup, $300 a month. Reputation Management is $195 a month. Websites are $3,000 lump or $180 a month. Ads are $300 a month minimum or 10% of spend. We can figure out together what makes sense, but you don't have to guess at the numbers."

**Proof:** Pricing page. Open it on the call.

**Follow-up:** "What's a comfortable monthly number for you for marketing — what feels right for a business your size?"

---

#### "Do I have to sign a long-term contract?"

**What they really mean:** "I don't want to be locked in if this doesn't work."

**Response:**

> "No lock-in on SEO, Ads, or Reputation Management — all month-to-month. The only commitment is the 12-month website subscription, and that exists so you can spread a custom build over monthly payments instead of paying $3,000 upfront. Complete the 12 months and you own the domain, the site, the assets. Or pay the $3,000 lump sum and there's no commitment at all."

**Proof:** The website subscription terms, in plain English, in the agreement.

**Follow-up:** "Which one fits better for how you'd want to budget this?"

---

#### "How quickly will I see results?"

**What they really mean:** "I need this to work before I run out of patience or money."

**Response:**

> "Different services on different timelines, and I'll be honest about each. Ads can start delivering leads in 1–2 weeks. A new website launches in 2–4 weeks. SEO is a 3–6 month build — it compounds, but it's not fast. Reputation Management starts generating new reviews in the first few weeks. I set timelines upfront and tell you when I'm ahead or behind them."

**Proof:** Specific past timelines. Document each project's launch date in your records to reference.

**Follow-up:** "What's the soonest you actually need leads — like, 'phone needs to ring' soonest?"

---

#### "Can you just handle everything? I don't want homework."

**What they really mean:** "I don't have time. Don't make me a project manager."

**Response:**

> "That's exactly what the managed services are for. You get reports, you get results, I handle everything in between. The only thing I need from you is a 30-minute kickoff call and occasional approval on creative or content. That's it."

**Proof:** Existing client onboarding flow. Reference time-spend per month from a current client.

**Follow-up:** "What's the most you'd want to be involved each month?"

---

#### "I don't have time to learn a new platform."

**What they really mean:** "I've been promised easy software before. It wasn't easy."

**Response:**

> "You don't have to. For Reputation Management clients, I configure and run the platform. You get a simple inbox to reply to your customers — that's the whole interface. No setup, no learning curve. If you want more access later, it's there. But you don't need it."

**Proof:** Demo the unified inbox view. Five seconds. That's it.

**Follow-up:** "Want me to show you what the inbox looks like? Takes 30 seconds."

---

#### "We already have a marketing person / agency."

**What they really mean:** "What makes you better than what I have?"

**Response:**

> "Fair question. The honest answer: I might not be. If your current setup is getting you the leads you need at a price you're comfortable with, stay there. The free audit will tell us either way. If it's working, I'll tell you. If there are gaps, I'll show you exactly where they are. You decide what to do about it."

**Proof:** Free audit is the proof. Don't argue — show.

**Follow-up:** "What are they doing for you, and how do you know it's working?"

---

#### "I tried [SEO / ads / website rebuild] before and it didn't work."

**What they really mean:** "I'm gun-shy. Tell me why this time is different without making me feel stupid."

**Response:**

> "Most of the time when SEO or ads don't work, it's because of one of three things — wrong service for the business, no follow-up infrastructure, or the agency wasn't doing the work they billed for. The audit will tell us which one happened to you. If the answer is 'this just isn't a fit for your business,' I'll say that."

**Proof:** Walk through their specific past attempt during the audit. Diagnose what went wrong.

**Follow-up:** "Walk me through what was happening — how long did you give it, what did you measure, what did the agency actually do?"

---

#### "Can you do [thing outside the four services]?"

**What they really mean:** "Are you a real partner or just a vendor?"

**Response:**

> "I focus on four things — websites, SEO, ads, reputation. If what you need is outside that, I'd rather refer you to someone who specializes in it than do a mediocre job to keep the relationship. Tell me what you're looking at and I'll either tell you yes, no, or here's who I'd send you to."

**Proof:** Build a referral list of trusted SWFL specialists for adjacent services (logo design, video, PR, traditional ads, branding).

**Follow-up:** "What's the actual outcome you're trying to get? Maybe one of the four covers it."

---

#### "Why should I work with someone local instead of a national agency?"

**What they really mean:** "Aren't bigger agencies better?"

**Response:**

> "For some kinds of marketing, sure — if you're a national brand. For local service businesses, the people ranking on your Google Map Pack and bidding on the same keywords as you are local. Knowing your verticals, your seasonality, your competitors by name — that doesn't fake well from a call center in another state. Plus, you can drive past my office. That matters when something goes sideways."

**Proof:** Local market knowledge. Bring up a specific local competitor or seasonality factor in their vertical during the audit.

**Follow-up:** "Have you worked with a national one before? How did that go?"

---

#### "What about AI? Are you using AI?"

**What they really mean:** Either (a) "I'm worried it'll be slop" or (b) "Is this future-proof?"

**Response:**

> "Honest answer: yes, I use modern tools to do research, content, and operations faster. But the strategy, the writing, the relationships — those are mine. The reason that matters to you isn't the technology. It's that you get sharper work and faster turnarounds without paying more for it. The AI is in my toolkit, not on your invoice."

**Proof:** Show before/after of a piece of content or a research report. Demonstrate, don't claim.

**Follow-up:** "Is the AI thing a concern or a curiosity for you?"

## 10. Competitive positioning

The four competitors you'll displace, ranked by how often you'll see them.

### vs. The local generalist agency (most common)

**Who they are:** A 5–25 person SWFL agency with a generic website, vague service tiers, junior account managers, and offshore production for the actual work. Often charges $1,500–$5,000/mo for what's effectively a templated WordPress site, basic GBP management, and a quarterly report.

**How they win:** Existing relationship inertia. The prospect already pays them and switching feels like work.

**How you win:**
- Founder-direct vs. account manager
- Modern stack (Next.js) vs. WordPress template
- Transparent pricing vs. opaque quotes
- Faster delivery via AI-augmented work
- Free audit shows specific gaps in what the agency is currently doing

**Talk track:**

> "I'm not going to bash your current agency — they may be doing fine. The audit will show specifically where the gaps are. If they're not real, I'll say that. If they are, you'll see them in plain English."

---

### vs. The national platform (BrightLocal, Yodle, Thryv, Podium, etc.)

**Who they are:** SaaS or platform companies offering a self-serve or lightly-managed local marketing toolkit. $200–$1,000/mo. The prospect or their office manager runs it.

**How they win:** Cheap. Familiar brand. "Includes everything."

**How you win:**
- Done-for-you vs. self-serve. The owner doesn't have to learn anything.
- Local market knowledge a national platform can't match
- Reputation Management ($195/mo) competes directly on price with no learning curve

**Talk track:**

> "Those tools are real and they work — for businesses with a marketing person who runs them daily. If you've got that, they're a good fit. If you don't, you're paying for software you won't use. Reputation Management is the same price as most of those tools, except I run it for you."

---

### vs. DIY / nephew with a Facebook page

**Who they are:** No agency. Owner does it themselves, or a family member helps, or a freelancer built a one-page site three years ago and disappeared.

**How they win:** Costs nothing. Feels manageable. "Word of mouth still works."

**How you win:**
- Show the cost of the leak. They're losing jobs they can't see.
- Free audit is non-threatening — no pitch attached
- Position as a partnership, not a takeover. Their nephew can keep posting; you'll handle the parts they don't.

**Talk track:**

> "Word of mouth is real and it's keeping you in business. The question is what you're missing. The audit will show specifically how many people are searching for your service every month in your area. You're not getting calls from any of them. That's the gap."

---

### vs. Doing nothing

**Who they are:** The prospect who agrees with everything on the call and then does nothing.

**How they win:** Status quo bias. Change feels risky. "What I have is working." Past agency burns make inaction feel safer than action.

**How you win:**
- Quantify the cost of waiting in their specific market
- Reduce risk: free audit, month-to-month services, no contract on most
- Lower the entry: Reputation Management at $195/mo is hard to argue against
- Make the next step small: a 15-minute audit, not a $5K commitment

**Talk track:**

> "You don't have to do anything. The free audit doesn't cost anything either way. Worst case, you find out you're already doing it right. Best case, we find $20K in jobs a year you're leaving on the table. That's the trade."

## 12. Deal pitfalls and warning signs

If you see two or more of these, slow down. The deal might be wrong.

| Warning sign | What it usually means |
|--------------|---------------------|
| They pushed for a discount before seeing the audit | Price-shopping, not partner-hunting |
| They've worked with 3+ agencies in the last 4 years | The pattern is them, not the agencies |
| The owner refuses to share GBP / ad account access | Control issue that won't get better |
| They want extensive customization on every service | Will eat your margins; not a fit for productized services |
| "I'll know when I see it" on creative direction | Endless revision cycles incoming |
| They ghost between discovery and close, then come back urgent | Rebound deal. Pricing should reflect the risk. |
| The decision-maker is "always traveling" | You're talking to someone who can't actually buy |
| They ask for free work "to see how you think" | Will never sign |
| They negotiate scope after the contract is signed | Will keep doing this forever |
| The check from the deposit doesn't clear in 5 days | Cash flow issue you'll never get out of |

**The one-question gut check before sending any agreement:**
"If this client is a problem in 90 days, will I regret signing them?" If yes, don't.

## 13. Operating cadence — the weekly rhythm

A founder-led pipeline lives or dies on whether you actually look at it weekly. This is the minimum cadence.

### Every Monday morning (30 minutes)

- [ ] Review every active deal — what stage is it in, what's the next action, when is it due?
- [ ] Move stalled deals (60+ days no movement) to long-term nurture
- [ ] Identify any deal that needs a touch this week and put it on the calendar
- [ ] Confirm 2–3 prospecting hours are blocked off this week

### Every Friday afternoon (15 minutes)

- [ ] Note one thing you learned this week — about a persona, an objection, a market dynamic
- [ ] Update this playbook if it's a real new pattern
- [ ] Send any outstanding follow-ups so nothing dangles into the weekend

### Every quarter (1 hour)

- [ ] Review every closed-won and closed-lost deal in the quarter
- [ ] What worked? What didn't?
- [ ] Update objection responses with new ones you've heard
- [ ] Refresh email templates if they're feeling stale
- [ ] Confirm pricing on the website matches what you're quoting

### What to track (minimum viable)

You don't need a CRM for the first 50 clients. A simple spreadsheet works. Track:

| Column | Why |
|--------|-----|
| Prospect / business name | Obvious |
| Persona match (Mike / James / Marcus / Other) | Pattern recognition |
| Source (referral / cold / inbound / network) | Tells you where to invest |
| Stage (Prospect / Audit Booked / Audit Done / Proposal / Closed) | Pipeline visibility |
| Last touch date | So you know who's overdue |
| Next action + date | Forces a next step on every deal |
| Notes | Their context, in their words |

When the pipeline gets too big for a sheet (probably around 30 active deals), graduate to Go High Level (you sell it — you should use it).
